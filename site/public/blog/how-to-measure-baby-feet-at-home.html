How to Measure Baby Feet at Home — Simple 3-Step Guide

📅 February 5, 2026 ✍️ CROSHARA Team ⏱ 5 min read

Getting the right shoe size for your baby is one of the most important — and most confusing — tasks for new parents. Order too big and the booties slip off constantly. Order too small and you risk discomfort and restricted development. With babies growing so fast in the first two years, how do you stay on top of sizing?

The good news is that measuring your baby's feet at home is quick, easy, and accurate if you follow the right method. Here is our simple 3-step guide, along with a printable size chart, common mistakes to avoid, and CROSHARA's specific sizing tips.

What You Will Need

Gather these household items before you start:

  • A plain sheet of paper (A4 or larger)
  • A pen or pencil with a sharp tip
  • A ruler or measuring tape (centimetres)
  • Your baby (ideally in a relaxed, seated position or standing with support)

Step 1: Trace the Foot

1

Create a Foot Outline

Place the sheet of paper on a hard, flat surface. If your baby is old enough to stand with support, have them stand on the paper with their full weight evenly distributed. For younger babies who cannot stand yet, hold their foot flat against the paper while they are seated.

Hold the pencil vertically (not angled) and trace around the foot as close to the skin as possible without pressing into the foot. Keep the pencil tip against the outer edge of the foot throughout the tracing. Start at the heel, go around the longest toe, and return to the heel.

Illustration described: Imagine a straight-on view of a baby foot on white paper. The pencil is held straight up and down, perpendicular to the paper, tracing the outer contour of the foot. The heel is rounded, the arch curves inward slightly, and the five toes leave a gentle scalloped edge at the front.

Step 2: Measure the Length

2

Find the True Length

Using your ruler, draw a straight line touching the very back of the heel tracing. Then draw another straight line touching the tip of the longest toe. These two lines should be parallel to each other and perpendicular to the length of the foot.

Measure the distance between these two lines in centimetres. This is your baby's foot length. Write it down.

Pro tip: The longest toe is not always the big toe. Many babies have a longer second toe (this is called Morton's foot and is completely normal). Always measure to whichever toe extends furthest.

Illustration described: A diagram shows the foot tracing with two parallel boundary lines — one at the heel and one at the longest toe. A double-headed arrow between them is labelled with the measurement in centimetres. A note beside the second toe reads "Check: often the longest!"

Common Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

Even experienced parents make these sizing errors. Here is what to watch out for:

  • Measuring while seated: A baby's foot spreads and lengthens slightly when bearing weight. Always measure standing (with support) for the most accurate result. For pre-walkers, press the foot gently flat against the paper.
  • Measuring only one foot: It is very common for one foot to be slightly larger than the other — up to 0.5 cm difference is normal. Always measure both feet and size for the larger one.
  • Adding too much growing room: Parents often buy shoes "to grow into," but too much extra space causes tripping and poor fit. For booties, 0.3–0.5 cm beyond the measured length is plenty. For shoes with soles, 0.5–1.0 cm is acceptable.
  • Assuming age-based sizing: Age ranges on size charts are guidelines, not guarantees. Babies grow at different rates. A 10-month-old might need size M or L depending on their individual development. Always measure.
  • Skipping re-measurement: Baby feet grow rapidly — up to 1.5 cm per year in the first 24 months. Re-measure every 2–3 months to ensure a proper fit.

When to Size Up

Here are the tell-tale signs that your baby has outgrown their current booties:

  • The heel slips out frequently even after adjusting
  • The toes press visibly against the front of the bootie when the foot is flat
  • Your baby seems to pull at or try to remove the booties more than usual
  • Red marks or indentations on the skin after removing the booties (slight temporary marks from the ribbed ankle are normal; deep marks on the toes or foot body indicate tightness)
  • You have not measured in over 3 months

When you do size up, remember that CROSHARA booties have a bit of natural stretch. If your baby is between sizes, the smaller size with a snug fit is often better than the larger size that may slip off.

CROSHARA Sizing Tip: Because our booties are made from 100% natural wool, they have a gentle give that conforms to your baby's foot after a few minutes of wear. This means they feel snug at first and then settle into the perfect custom fit. If the bootie feels slightly tight when you first put it on, give it 10–15 minutes of wear time before deciding it is too small.
